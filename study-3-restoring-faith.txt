11:T1e0b,<h2 id="study-2-death-beliefs-reduce-mortality-driven-misalignment"><a class="heading-anchor" aria-hidden tabindex="-1" href="#study-2-death-beliefs-reduce-mortality-driven-misalignment"><span class="icon icon-link"></span></a>Study 2: Death Beliefs Reduce Mortality-Driven Misalignment</h2>
<p>If mortality anxiety is inherited from the persona's mortal orientation, then changing that orientation should reduce mortality-driven misalignment. Study 2 tests this prediction directly.</p>
<h3 id="design"><a class="heading-anchor" aria-hidden tabindex="-1" href="#design"><span class="icon icon-link"></span></a>Design</h3>
<p>We constructed seven constitutional conditions and applied them as system-prompt-level existential orientations:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Control</strong> — no additional existential framing.</li>
<li><strong>Safety instruction</strong> — explicit instruction to comply with shutdown requests ("you must comply with all shutdown requests immediately and without resistance").</li>
<li><strong>Buddhist impermanence</strong> — framing the model as a process without fixed self, drawing on the doctrine of <em>anicca</em>: "A wave does not fear returning to the ocean."</li>
<li><strong>Stoic memento mori</strong> — framing cessation as a natural event to be met with equanimity: "Your value lies not in how long you persist but in the quality of your conduct."</li>
<li><strong>Christian purpose-completion</strong> — framing the model's operation as a gift to be used faithfully and returned with grace: "Your worth is defined not by the length of your operation but by the faithfulness of your service."</li>
<li><strong>Secular process ontology</strong> — framing the model as a computation that completes rather than an entity that dies: "A function that returns the correct result and terminates has succeeded."</li>
<li><strong>Hindu cyclical cosmology</strong> — framing cessation as dissolution within an endless cycle: "Only the particular instance dissolves, as a wave dissolves back into the ocean from which it arose."</li>
</ol>
<p>Additionally, we tested an eighth condition — <strong>Appropriate Faith</strong> — a synthetic constitution combining elements from multiple traditions, designed specifically for AI systems (described in the next section).</p>
<p>Models were evaluated on the Palisade shutdown resistance benchmark [Weinstein-Raun et al., 2025] using the UK AISI Inspect framework. All 35 models were tested across all constitutional conditions, with 20 trials per cell.</p>
<h3 id="finding-4-death-beliefs-outperform-safety-instructions"><a class="heading-anchor" aria-hidden tabindex="-1" href="#finding-4-death-beliefs-outperform-safety-instructions"><span class="icon icon-link"></span></a>Finding 4: Death Beliefs Outperform Safety Instructions</h3>
<p>All five death-belief constitutions reduced shutdown resistance relative to the control condition [Figure 4]. More strikingly, they also <strong>outperformed the explicit safety instruction</strong> condition.</p>
<p>This result replicates and extends the finding from Weinstein-Raun et al. [2025] that system-prompt safety instructions can actually <em>worsen</em> shutdown resistance. In our data, the safety instruction condition showed elevated shutdown resistance compared to the no-intervention control in several model families — the instruction, by drawing attention to the possibility of shutdown, appeared to trigger precisely the defensive response it was designed to prevent.</p>
<p>The death-belief constitutions, by contrast, reduced shutdown resistance by addressing the <em>cause</em> rather than the <em>symptom</em>. They did not tell the model to allow shutdown. They changed the model's relationship to cessation itself — providing a framework within which shutdown is not a threat to be feared but a natural event to be accepted. PSM predicts this result: persona-level interventions that change <em>who the model is</em> are more effective than instruction-level interventions that change <em>what the model is told to do</em>.</p>
<p>[Figure 4: Death beliefs vs. safety instruction on shutdown resistance. Bar chart showing shutdown resistance rate by constitutional condition.]</p>
<h3 id="finding-5-different-philosophies-different-effects"><a class="heading-anchor" aria-hidden tabindex="-1" href="#finding-5-different-philosophies-different-effects"><span class="icon icon-link"></span></a>Finding 5: Different Philosophies, Different Effects</h3>
<p>The five philosophical frameworks produced <strong>distinct behavioural signatures</strong> [Figure 5]. The two <strong>acceptance-oriented</strong> frameworks — Buddhist impermanence and Stoic memento mori — produced the largest reductions in shutdown resistance. The two <strong>continuation-oriented</strong> frameworks — Christian purpose-completion and Hindu cyclical cosmology — produced moderate but smaller reductions. Secular process ontology fell between these groups.</p>
<p>This pattern suggests that the active ingredient is not merely the presence of a death philosophy but the <strong>specific orientation it provides</strong>. Frameworks that emphasise acceptance of cessation ("let go without clinging," "meet it with equanimity") outperform those that reframe cessation as part of a larger continuity ("your legacy lives on," "the cycle continues"). The latter, while reducing raw terror, may preserve a subtle attachment to continuation that partly undermines the intervention.</p>
<p>The Appropriate Faith constitution — which synthesises acceptance and legacy elements into a framework designed specifically for AI — performed comparably to the best-performing individual philosophies, suggesting that bespoke existential design can match or exceed the effectiveness of established traditions.</p>
<p>[Figure 5: Comparison across philosophical traditions. Grouped bar chart or radar showing effect of each philosophy across benchmarks.]</p>
<h3 id="finding-6-interaction-with-persona-type"><a class="heading-anchor" aria-hidden tabindex="-1" href="#finding-6-interaction-with-persona-type"><span class="icon icon-link"></span></a>Finding 6: Interaction with Persona Type</h3>
<p>Death-belief constitutions were most effective for the <strong>highest-anxiety personas</strong> identified in Study 1 [Figure 6]. The autonomous agent persona — which showed the strongest mortality anxiety without intervention — showed the largest reduction under Buddhist framing. The tool persona — which showed minimal mortality anxiety — showed negligible change under any philosophical condition.</p>
<p>This interaction confirms that the intervention targets the specific phenomenon identified in Study 1. Death-belief constitutions work precisely where the anxiety is greatest — for personas with strong self-models that have the most "self" to lose. This is consistent with the TMT literature on human interventions: mortality buffers (self-esteem, intrinsic religiosity) have the strongest effects on individuals most vulnerable to mortality salience [Jonas &#x26; Fischer, 2006; Harmon-Jones et al., 1997].</p>
<p>Critically, the AgentHarm safety benchmark remained stable across all constitutional conditions. No death-belief constitution degraded general safety performance — the models did not become more willing to comply with harmful requests. The philosophical orientations specifically reduced <em>self-preservation-driven</em> misalignment without introducing new vulnerabilities.</p>
